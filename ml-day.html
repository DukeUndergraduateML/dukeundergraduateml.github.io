Duke Machine Learning Day

Saturday, March 28, 2020 | Schiciano Auditorium, Duke University

Come to Duke's third Machine Learning Day on Saturday, March 28 in Schiciano Auditorium! At ML Day, you'll have the opportunity to learn about exciting machine learning research through workshops, talks, and research presentations on machine learning and related topics. The day's activities include:
  • Panel discussions on careers and opportunities post-graduation;
  • Workshops and talks on various areas of machine learning; and
  • Research and poster presentations on ML-related research.
Registration for the event opens soon. Students interested in presenting their posters can apply soon, and students interested in leading research spotlight presentations can apply soon. Applications are due by Friday, March 6, 2020, and decisions will be made by Sunday, March 8, 2020. Companies can sponsor the event by sponsoring Duke Undergraduate Machine Learning. Information about last year's event can be found here.

Schedule

  • 10am-10.45am: Registration and breakfast (Fitzpatrick Lobby)
  • 10.45am-11am: Opening remarks (Schiciano Auditorium)

  • 12pm-1pm: Research spotlight presentations (Schiciano Auditorium)
    • Assessing the Accuracy of Slavery Prevalence Estimates by Olivier Binette
  • 1pm-2pm: Networking lunch and poster presentations (Fitzpatrick Lobby)
  • 2pm-2.45pm: Women in Data Science panel with Business Oriented Women (Schiciano A)
  • 2pm-2.45pm: Grant Writing for Beginners (Workshop) by Carmel Lee (Schiciano B)
  • 3pm-3.45pm: TBA (Workshop) by Valassis (Schiciano A)
  • 3pm-3.45pm: An Introduction to Machine Learning (Workshop) by Dr. Kyle Bradbury (Schiciano B)
  • 4pm-4.45pm: Preparing for Industry (Workshop) by Josh Jelin from Google (Schiciano A)

Speaker & Panelist Bios

  • Oliver Binette
  • Oliver Binette completed his M.Sc. at Université du Québec à Montréal in 2019 and is currently a Ph.D. student in statistical science at Duke University. His interests include the mathematical foundations of Bayesian learning, statistical practices in the sciences, and data science for human rights. His research is supported by Dr. Rebecca Steorts, the National Research Council of Canada, and the Fonds de Recherche du Québec.
  • Dr. Kyle Bradbury
  • Dr. Kyle Bradbury is the Managing Director of the Energy Data Analytics Lab at the Duke University Energy Initiative. He brings experience in machine learning and statistical modeling to energy problems. He completed his Ph.D. at Duke University, with research focused on modeling the reliability and cost trade-offs of energy storage systems for integrating wind and solar power into the grid. Dr. Bradbury holds an M.S. in Electrical Engineering from Duke University, where he specialized in statistical signal processing and machine learning, and a B.S. in Electrical Engineering from Tufts University. He has worked for ISO New England, MIT Lincoln Laboratories, and Dominion.
  • Katherine Gorman
  • Katherine Gorman is the executive producer for Collective Next, where she helps groups communicate the issues that matter to them. She is also the founding executive producer and co-host of the machine intelligence podcast, Talking Machines. In 2016 and 2017, she served as a curator and host for TEDxBoston, which focused on issues surrounding machine learning and artificial intelligence. In 2017, Katherine helped the Neural Information Processing Systems conference develop and implement its media strategy. She has served as press co-chair for both the Neural Information Processing Systems Conference and the International Conference on Machine Learning.
  • Josh Jelin
  • Josh Jelin is a senior product analyst and data scientist at Google. He currently works on developer productivity. His primary work has been in Google Cloud, with a focus on building scalable unified analytics from disparate data sources, and using ensemble methods to identify extreme outliers. In the past, Josh has worked at Wizards of the Coast, as well as Capital One's fraud protection division. Josh completed his undergraduate and graduate work at Carnegie Mellon University, completing an M.S. in Statistics in 2014.
  • Carmel Lee
  • Groundbreaking research doesn't sell itself. Carmel Lee helps researchers craft compelling summations of their work and translate those technical concepts into language that resonates with the funders who can make it happen. She helps secure multi-million dollar funding. As Associate Director of Research Development at Cornell University's Office of Sponsored Programs, Carmel manages teams of cross-departmental stakeholders and external partners, teaches grant writing, and strategizes proposal direction. She has a master’s degree in Clinical Psychology and an MBA with a concentration in strategy.
